  The Constitution of India is the foundational governing document of the Republic of India. Adopted by the Constituent Assembly on November 26, 1949, it came into force on January 26, 1950. It establishes the framework for a sovereign, socialist, secular, democratic republic and outlines the fundamental political code, structure, procedures, powers, and duties of government institutions, while also setting out fundamental rights, directive principles, and the duties of citizens. TERMINOLOGY:
  - State :: As defined in Article 12 for the purpose of Fundamental Rights, it includes the Government and Parliament of India, the Government and the Legislature of each of the States, and all local or other authorities within the territory of India or under the control of the Government of India.
  - Law :: As defined in Article 13, it includes any Ordinance, order, bye-law, rule, regulation, notification, custom, or usage having in the territory of India the force of law.
  - Writ :: A formal written order issued by a court. Articles 32 and 226 of the Constitution empower the Supreme Court and High Courts, respectively, to issue writs of habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto, and certiorari for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights and for any other purpose.
  - Federalism :: A system of government in which power is divided between a central authority and constituent political units, such as states. The Indian Constitution establishes a quasi-federal structure, with a clear division of legislative powers detailed in the Seventh Schedule.
  - Separation of Powers :: The constitutional principle that divides governmental authority into three branches: the legislative (makes laws), the executive (implements laws), and the judicial (interprets laws). This is a foundational feature of the Indian governmental structure.
  - Judicial Review :: The power of the judiciary, particularly the Supreme Court and High Courts, to examine the constitutionality of legislative enactments and executive orders of both the Central and State governments. Any law or order found to be in violation of the Constitution can be declared null and void.
  - Pith and Substance :: A legal doctrine used to determine which head of power (Union, State, or Concurrent List) a particular piece of legislation falls under. The court looks at the true nature and character of the legislation to ascertain its "pith and substance."
